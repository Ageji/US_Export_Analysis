# US Export Financing Analysis
### Project Overview
This project explores how the U.S. government supports businesses through export financing. Using historical financing data, the analysis examines where funding goes, which industries and businesses receive the most support, and how financing changes over time.

It combines Python to prepare and analyze the data with Power BI to create an interactive dashboard. The dashboard allows users to explore financing trends, compare different regions and industries, and monitor key performance indicators through easy-to-understand visualizations.

### Project Objectives
The main goals of this project were to:
- Understand how U.S. export financing is distributed.
- Identify the countries, industries, and businesses receiving the most financing.
- Monitor overall financing performance using key metrics.
- Explore trends in financing over time.
- Build an interactive dashboard that helps users answer business questions quickly.

  ### Findings
  ### Executive Overview
This dashboard tracks U.S. government-backed loans and guarantees given to help American companies sell their products overseas.
- Over 52,000 deals were recorded and almost all of them were approved  only 6 were ever rejected.
- The government approved a total of $264 billion in financing, of which $223 billion has already been paid out.
- About $17.6 billion is still outstanding, meaning it has been promised but not yet fully repaid.
- Activity was highest in 2012 with over 4,000 deals per year. Since then the number of deals has fallen every year, dropping to just 1,383 in 2025.
- Financing amounts followed the same pattern peaking at $35.8 billion in 2012, dropping sharply through 2017, and settling at around $5 to $9 billion per year in recent years.

  ### Geographic Analysis
This section looks at which countries, states, and cities are most involved in U.S. export financing.
- The United States itself receives the most financing ($34.7 billion) because many deals support exports that pass through or benefit domestic operations.
- Mexico is the largest overseas recipient with $17.6 billion, reflecting the strong trade relationship between the two countries.
- Other major recipients include India ($10.4 billion), Turkey ($9.7 billion), the United Arab Emirates ($9 billion), and Saudi Arabia ($7.9 billion).
- Within the U.S., Houston is the biggest hub with 357 exporting companies, mainly in energy. Miami and South Florida follow closely, driven by trade with Latin America.
- Saudi Arabia has the highest amount of financing that is still active and unpaid ($3.5 billion), suggesting large long-term deals are still running.
- Mexico has had the most cancelled deals (94), followed by the United States (50) and China (29).

  ### Industry & Program Analysis
This section shows which industries and types of financing programs are used the most.
- Aerospace and Defense is by far the biggest industry, receiving $87.7 billion about one third of all financing. This is largely driven by aircraft sales, particularly by Boeing.
- Energy is the third largest industry at $35.2 billion, followed by Professional Services, Technology, Industrial Machinery, and Agriculture.
- Although Aerospace receives the most money, Industrial Machinery has the highest number of individual deals (6,002), meaning it involves many smaller transactions.
- The most common type of financing is a Guarantee ($112.4 billion), where the government promises to cover the lender if a borrower defaults. This is preferred because it reduces risk for banks without requiring the government to lend money directly.
- Export Lines of Credit are the most used policy type at $37.6 billion, followed by other flexible credit tools.

### Exporter & Business Analysis
This section looks at which companies export the most and which banks provide the financing.
- Boeing is the single largest exporter, receiving $77.7 billion in financing nearly 30 cents of every dollar approved goes to Boeing.
- Thousands of smaller businesses are grouped together under 'Multiple Exporters', collectively receiving $47.7 billion across over 1,500 deals.
- Energy companies like Anadarko Petroleum and Enerflex average the largest individual deal sizes, with some deals worth $500 million to $1 billion each.
- Ex-Im Bank provides the most financing by value ($49.6 billion), while Wells Fargo handles the most individual deals (887). This shows that government banks handle the big deals while commercial banks deal with volume.
- The program also supports smaller and underrepresented businesses $61.4 billion went to small businesses, $7.1 billion to minority-owned businesses, and $4.3 billion to women-owned businesses.

  ### Recommendations
1. Bring in more exporters beyond Boeing
Almost 30% of all financing goes to one company. There is a large opportunity to grow the program by actively supporting more mid-sized exporters across different industries. Reaching out to companies in Technology, Agriculture, and Industrial Machinery sectors that already have high deal numbers but lower financing would spread the benefits more widely and reduce dependence on a single exporter.

2. Revive deal activity through Working Capital programs
Deal volume has fallen sharply since 2012. Working Capital is the most practical tool for smaller exporters who need upfront funding to fulfil orders, yet it is the smallest of the four programs. Investing in and promoting Working Capital financing is one of the most direct ways to bring more businesses into the program and reverse the decline in deal numbers.

3.  Open up more markets outside the U.S. and Mexico
Most deals are concentrated in just two countries. Regions like Southeast Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and South America are underrepresented despite strong demand for U.S. goods. Expanding into these markets would create new revenue streams and give American exporters access to faster-growing economies.
